_______   PROJECT DETAILS

Understanding the project.

About

It's a hyperlocal marketplace tailored for the African Leadership University students. It connects (student) vendors with (student and faculty members) buyers within the same campus environment, enabling fast, secure, and convenient transactions for products like snacks, food, drinks, and delicacies.

Problem Statement

Many university students, particularly at the African Leadership University (ALU), seek ways to earn a livelihood while managing their studies. Some students have started small businesses to support themselves financially, particularly in the food and service industry. However, these businesses are often promoted and managed through informal channels, primarily WhatsApp groups. The current system leads to cluttered communication as WhatsApp groups are overwhelmed by vendor promotions, lack a structured marketplace for easy product discovery, and result in inefficient operations where vendors struggle to manage offerings and orders, limiting their business growth.

RESEARCH

_______   KEY OBSERVATIONS

Asking the right questions.

Research goals
We set out to understand how students buy and sell on campus, what tools they currently use, and what pain points exist in their day-to-day commerce interactions. Our goal was to build a platform that simplifies both selling and buying without taking away the spontaneity and informality of campus-based trading.
Student Vendor Experience

Our market research revealed that most student entrepreneurs relied heavily on informal channels like WhatsApp group chats (e.g., ALU Kigali, ALU 9ja) and personal Instagram stories to promote their products. While these platforms helped them reach peers, they lacked the structure and tools needed to run a smooth business. Vendors frequently faced challenges with building trust among buyers, coordinating payments manually, and arranging deliveries efficiently. Many expressed the need for a more organized system. They wanted a platform where they could professionally list their products, receive and manage orders, and get instant notifications to stay on top of their sales.

Student & Faculty Buyer Experience

Many buyers struggled with the uncertainty of purchasing from multiple campus vendors. They often couldn’t confirm whether a product was truly in stock or when it would be delivered. This created frustration and delays, especially for students relying on timely access to essential items. What they needed was a reliable platform where they could easily browse different vendors, compare product offerings, check vendor ratings, and place orders securely. One of the most common complaints was the lack of transparency. Buyers frequently encountered inconsistent pricing and unclear availability, which made it difficult to plan purchases confidently

Software Design

For this to be a success, the platform needed to strike a careful balance between professionalism and a casual, student-friendly experience. It had to be functional and reliable - capable of tracking orders, managing inventory, and handling multiple users - while still maintaining a relaxed, peer-to-peer vibe that felt natural for student communities. Real-time updates were seen as must-have to keep both buyers and sellers informed without delay. Additionally, since many students accessed the platform using limited mobile data, ensuring fast load times and optimized performance was a top priority for usability on campus.

Question areas
I would often spend time listening to and observing students across the African Leadership campus, especially those running small businesses, to better understand how they currently sell their products and the hurdles they face. These insights allowed me to better understand the challenges they face and the need for a structured system like Campus Cart. By having answers to questions about trust issues, payment coordination, and delivery logistics, the team was able to prioritize features that would empower student vendors without losing the informal, peer-to-peer feel that makes student commerce unique.

_______   SOFTWARE BLUEPRINT

System Workflows.

Design Philosophy
I prioritized modular design and mobile-first development while designing the Campus Cart architecture. Campus Cart was built to work well on low-end devices with slow internet speeds. The backend emphasized data consistency and fault tolerance, particularly for order processing and inventory sync.
Core Interactions
Campus Cart was designed to offer an easy user experience by supporting two key user roles — vendors and buyers — within a single, unified platform. The core interactions were intentionally structured to feel natural and efficient, helping users navigate their objectives (listing & ordering) with minimal friction. Below are the main interactions that drive the user experience:
Product Discovery & Search
Buyers can browse through a range of vendor listings, with the ability to filter by categories, availability, and pricing.
Order Placement & Checkout
Once a buyer selects items, they can add them to a shopping cart and proceed to place an order.
Store Management
Vendors have access to a personalized dashboard where they can manage their product listings.
System Design and Algorithms
Building Campus Cart required deliberate system design choices and algorithms that prioritized real-time responsiveness, scalability, and ease of integration. I chose Firebase as the backend platform due to its ability to handle real-time data, user authentication, and flexible NoSQL data modeling.

CONCLUSIONS

_______   KEY FINDINGS

Measuring success.

"We built Campus Cart to give students a real marketplace. A place for fast, accessible, and easy buying and selling - owned by their community."

What we did well

Designing Campus Cart's user experience was one of the most rewarding aspects of the project. I collaborated with a team that understood the nuances of campus life and the need for efficiency. We structured the core interactions around two user roles: vendors and buyers, but intentionally allowed for fluid role switching. Users could act as buyers or vendors without needing separate accounts, and this dual-role feature had no complex implementation. By default, a user is a buyer; then, when the buyer sets up their store, they gain the functionality of being a vendor as well. This role management handles local state, ensuring an easy transition without interrupting the session or reloading the app. The interaction flow itself mirrored familiar e-commerce experiences but was stripped down to suit the ALU environment.

What I would do differently

In hindsight, I would have invested more time in user testing before finalizing the core workflows. While our dual-role worked as expected on the technical side, some users expressed confusion when trying to perform both buyer and vendor actions in quick succession. Implementing clear visual cues or onboarding walkthroughs could have improved clarity during early adoption.

Lastly, another area I would revisit is the shopping cart logic. We assumed synchronous ordering behavior, but in practice, vendors sometimes changed product availability right after a buyer added an item to their cart. This led to race conditions that we resolved later with additional real-time validation. In the future, I’d prioritize pre-check hooks or reservation-based logic to minimize frustration during checkout.
