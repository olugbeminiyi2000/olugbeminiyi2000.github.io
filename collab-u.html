_______   PROJECT DETAILS

Understanding the project.

About

It's a student-centric platform designed to improve collaboration and communication among students at African Leadership University (ALU) by enabling them to interact, share resources, and collaborate on projects in real time.

Problem Statement

ALU students often struggle to manage group projects effectively due to the lack of affordable and integrated tools. While general project management platforms like Trello and Slack exist, their free versions have limitations such as restricted features and storage. These tools are also not designed for academic collaboration, making them unsuitable for ALU students working on group projects.

_______   KEY OBSERVATIONS

Asking the right questions.

Research goals
To build a platform that genuinely meets the needs of ALU students, our research focused on understanding how students and faculty currently manage group projects, identifying pain points, and clarifying feature requirements. The research for CollabU was focused on understanding the collaboration gaps in remote learning.
Students Experience

Through user research, we discovered that students often felt overwhelmed by the number of disconnected tools they had to use for collaboration. The chat apps, file-sharing services, note-taking platforms, and group schedulers were becoming too much. This fragmented experience made it harder for students to stay on top of deadlines, discussions, and shared materials. Many students expressed a strong desire for a platform where everything they needed for group work, communication, files, notes, and task tracking was available in one place. They didn’t want complexity; they wanted something simple and tailored to how they already work and study in groups.

Educator Experience

The challenge wasn’t just to build features for facilitators; it was understanding why those features would matter in their day-to-day teaching. Educators were juggling multiple tools, emails, and learning systems to manage classes and monitor student collaboration. Many shared frustrations around not being able to easily track participation or provide timely feedback in group assignments. They needed a solution that could help with student oversight without adding more to their already heavy workload. Our research emphasized the need to balance functionality with simplicity, allowing facilitators to support collaboration while staying focused on helping with the learning experience.

Software Design

A platform can’t just work; it has to work well for the people who use it every day. In designing CollabU, we realized that students and faculty weren’t looking for every feature under the sun. They wanted a tool that felt natural from the start, with a clean interface and clear paths to action. That meant building real-time functionality like chat, notes, and notifications without making the experience feel cluttered or confusing. The software had to adapt to different users’ needs: flexible enough for creative collaboration but structured enough for academic accountability. This drove our decision to keep the backend lean, the UI clear, and the user flow frictionless.

Question areas
I would often conduct casual interviews and structured feedback sessions with students across disciplines to uncover how they collaborated on group projects, communicated with peers, and managed shared resources. These conversations revealed the fragmented nature of their workflows. This patchwork made group work chaotic, especially when members were remote or had conflicting schedules. By asking the right questions and listening closely to their stories, we began to understand not only what tools they used, but also how those tools made them feel frustrated, scattered, and unsupported.

Cons

Pros

____ Notifications help users stay on top of conversations

Cons

____ Not built for project tracking or task management
____ No shared dashboard for project overviews

_______   SOFTWARE BLUEPRINT

System Workflows.

Design Philosophy
I prioritized modular design patterns that allowed for feature decoupling and easy iteration. The platform was built web-first to accommodate students who primarily access digital tools via PCs. I focused on keeping the UI lightweight and responsive, ensuring that the core experience - communication, collaboration, and file sharing - was simple even on slow internet connections. CollabU was architected with a deep understanding of the ALU student environment, which involves balancing academic workload, group projects, and limited digital infrastructure.
Core Interactions
CollabU was designed to serve both students and educators in collaborative learning environments. The goal was to reduce tool-switching and centralize collaboration, making academic project work smoother and more aligned with ALU’s group-based learning philosophy.
System Design and Algorithms
CollabU’s backend architecture was intentionally kept lightweight and flexible using a custom JSON server to simulate RESTful API behavior during development and testing. The decision to use JSON Server allowed us to rapidly prototype endpoints and model key resources such as groups, users, comments, files, and ⁣ versions in a flat, human-readable structure.

CONCLUSIONS

_______   KEY FINDINGS

Measuring success.

"CollabU was built to give students and educators a home for real collaboration. A space where teamwork feels natural, communication flows easily, and everyone can contribute through one platform."

What we did well

CollabU’s collaborative experience was deeply rewarding. We focused on enabling smooth teamwork by unifying messaging, file sharing, and task tracking into one platform. Rather than mimic corporate tools, we grounded the experience in how students and educators naturally interact on campus. A key success was the ability to create group spaces that supported real-time discussions, shared resources, and transparent contribution tracking. We also built in a lightweight mechanism for educators to review group progress without adding to their workload. Because CollabU was built with ALU students in mind, every feature prioritized low bandwidth, fast access, and web-friendly interfaces.

What I would do differently

Looking back, I would dedicate more time to refining the onboarding experience for new users. While the platform was easy to use for those familiar with digital tools, some first-time users needed guidance on setting up groups or managing files. Adding a simple in-app walkthrough or visual helper could have helped users discover features more confidently from the start.

Another aspect worth improving is the chat experience. We initially used basic useEffect polling to fetch messages, which led to lag and frequent re-renders. A real-time approach using WebSockets or a service like Firebase would have made communication faster, smoother, and more reliable, especially when users were active across multiple groups.
